Lesson 4: Positive and Negative Decimals

Learning Outcome

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to identify, represent, and compare positive and negative decimals.

What are decimals?

A decimal is another way of writing a fraction whose denominator is a power of 10.

Examples:

  • \(0.5 = \frac{5}{10}\)
  • \(0.25 = \frac{25}{100}\)
  • \(1.2 = \frac{12}{10}\)

Positive and negative decimals

  • A positive decimal has a value greater than zero. Example: \(0.7\)
  • A negative decimal has a value less than zero. Example: \(-0.7\)

The negative sign shows that the value is below zero.

Decimals on a number line

Decimals can be placed on a number line just like integers and fractions.

  • Positive decimals are to the right of zero.
  • Negative decimals are to the left of zero.

Example order:

\(-1.2 < -0.4 < 0 < 0.6 < 1.3\)

Comparing positive and negative decimals

  • Any positive decimal is greater than any negative decimal.
  • Among negative decimals, the one closer to zero is greater.

Examples:

  • \(0.3 > -0.3\)
  • \(-0.2 > -0.8\)

Common Mistakes

Mistake: Thinking \(-0.9\) is greater than \(-0.3\) because 9 is greater than 3.

Correct understanding: \(-0.3\) is greater because it is closer to zero.

Mini Practice

  1. State whether each decimal is positive or negative: \(-1.5, 0.8\)
  2. Arrange \(-0.6, 0.4, -1.2\) in ascending order.
  3. Which is greater: \(-0.7\) or \(-0.2\)? Explain.

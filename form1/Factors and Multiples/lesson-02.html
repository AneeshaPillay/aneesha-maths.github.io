Lesson 2: Multiples, Common Multiples & Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

Learning Outcome

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to list multiples of a number, find common multiples, and determine the Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) of two or three numbers.

What are multiples?

A multiple of a number is the result of multiplying that number by a whole number.

Example: Multiples of \(4\) are:

\(4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, \dots\)

We can also write multiples of \(4\) as: \(4\times1,\ 4\times2,\ 4\times3,\ 4\times4,\ \dots\)

Common multiples

Common multiples are multiples that appear in the lists of both numbers.

Example: Find common multiples of \(3\) and \(5\).

Multiples of \(3\): \(3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27, 30, \dots\)

Multiples of \(5\): \(5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, \dots\)

Common multiples: \(15, 30, 45, \dots\)

Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

The Lowest Common Multiple (LCM) is the smallest number that is a multiple of all the given numbers.

Example: LCM of \(3\) and \(5\) is \(15\).

Method A: Listing multiples (good for small numbers)

Steps:

  1. List multiples of each number.
  2. Find the first number that appears in both lists.
  3. That first common multiple is the LCM.

Example: Find LCM of \(4\) and \(6\).

Multiples of \(4\): \(4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, \dots\)

Multiples of \(6\): \(6, 12, 18, 24, 30, \dots\)

LCM = \(12\)

Method B: Prime factorisation (best method)

Steps:

  1. Write each number as a product of prime factors.
  2. List all prime factors needed to build both numbers.
  3. Use the highest power of each prime factor.
  4. Multiply them to get the LCM.

Example: Find LCM of \(12\) and \(18\).

\(12 = 2^2 \times 3\)

\(18 = 2 \times 3^2\)

Take highest powers: \(2^2\) and \(3^2\)

LCM = \(2^2 \times 3^2 = 36\)

Method C: Ladder Method (division method)

The ladder method is a neat way to find the LCM by dividing using prime numbers.

Steps:

  1. Write the numbers side by side.
  2. Divide by a prime number that can divide at least one of the numbers.
  3. If a number cannot be divided, bring it down unchanged.
  4. Continue until all numbers become \(1\).
  5. Multiply all the numbers on the left to get the LCM.

Example: Find the LCM of \(12\) and \(18\).

2 | 12   18
2 |  6    9
3 |  3    9
3 |  1    3
  |  1    1

Multiply the divisors: \(2 \times 2 \times 3 \times 3 = 36\)

LCM = \(36\)

Reminder: For LCM, you keep dividing until every number becomes \(1\).

Try These

1. List the first 6 multiples of \(7\).

2. Find the common multiples of \(4\) and \(10\).

3. Find the LCM of \(6\) and \(8\).

4. Find the LCM of \(9\) and \(12\).

5. Find the LCM of \(6,\ 10,\ 15\).

Show Answers

1. \(7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42\)

2. Common multiples include \(20, 40, 60, \dots\) (lowest is \(20\))

3. LCM\((6,8)=24\)

4. LCM\((9,12)=36\)

5. LCM\((6,10,15)=30\)

Lesson 3.1: Squares and Square Roots

Learning Outcome

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to find squares and square roots of whole numbers and identify perfect squares.

Square Numbers

A square number is a number obtained by multiplying a whole number by itself.

If the number is \(n\), then its square is: \[ n^2 = n \times n \]

Examples:

  • \(4^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16\)
  • \(7^2 = 7 \times 7 = 49\)
  • \(10^2 = 10 \times 10 = 100\)

Squaring Negative Numbers

When a negative number is squared, the answer is positive.

  • \((-5)^2 = (-5)(-5) = 25\)
  • \((-8)^2 = 64\)

Be careful with brackets:

  • \((-5)^2 = 25\)
  • \(-5^2 = -(5^2) = -25\)

Square Roots

The square root of a number is the value that, when multiplied by itself, gives the original number.

The square root symbol is \(\sqrt{\ \ }\).

Examples:

  • \(\sqrt{16} = 4\), because \(4^2 = 16\)
  • \(\sqrt{49} = 7\)
  • \(\sqrt{100} = 10\)

Perfect Squares

A perfect square is a number that can be written as the square of a whole number.

Examples of perfect squares:

\(1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100\)

Numbers such as 20, 50 and 90 are not perfect squares.

Estimating Square Roots

If a number is not a perfect square, we can estimate its square root by finding the nearest perfect squares.

Example: Estimate \(\sqrt{60}\).

Since \(49 = 7^2\) and \(64 = 8^2\), \[ 7 < \sqrt{60} < 8 \]
